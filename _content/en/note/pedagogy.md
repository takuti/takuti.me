There are many educational practices out there.

Recognizing the complexity, communities proposed a methodology called [Design-Based Research](https://edtechbooks.org/education_research/design_based_research) (DBR). In contrast to traditional studies conducted in controlled laboratory settings, DBR contextualizes academic studies by placing researchers as collaborative agents with subjects. Through the field collaboration and iterative design and feedback process, DBR aims to yield authentic and useful knowledge in classroom practices.

As such, education scholars are working hard to streamline best practices, make tactics actionable, and keep things up-to-date. And, especially in the Global North, teachers are very much aware of what is desired in theory.

To the best of my knowledge, the following components signify modern pedagogy.

- **Digital skills**, as society actively invests in infrastructure development and skill training projects. It is necessary to respond to the technological advancement in classrooms.
- **Cultural understanding** to overcome realistic constraints, acknowledge local contexts, and develop long-lasting, tool-agnostic skills in the information age. In fact, [digital skills may be optional](/note/offline-learning) to achieve an educational goal.
- **Agency** for what Brazilian educator Paulo Freire called critical consciousness and problem-posing education, which encourage learners to actively participate in educational projects. It is also the key to [bridge the third-level of the digital divide](/note/digital-divide).

The [incompleteness of computer education in low-income countries](/note/computer-education-in-malawi) can be explained by the lack of cultural understanding and learners' agency, for example.

In the meantime, during the COVID-19 pandemic, digitally unprepared populations were unable to continue their learning journey as the rest of the world shifted to online, though this constraint unlocked another avenue for [low-tech innovations](https://ab.takuti.me/p/low-tech-approach-for-mass-remote-education).

So, education will bring a net-positive impact, as long as we digitize tools (e.g., tablets, LMS), make topics relevant to students' daily lives, and incorporate participatory and interactive exercises into classrooms (e.g., games, group discussion).

Most futuristic narratives stop here. But, really?

I recently had the opportunity to participate in a "pedagogically correct" classroom for my French learning, and honestly, I wouldn't like it.

### Best practices are not for everyone

Well, my classmates and our teacher are great. They are very nice and kind people, and the level of the class seems well-aligned with my level. Yet, there are three foundational challenges apart from it.

First, digital tools make courses highly predictable.

Our teacher actively uses technology. For example, we have a Moodle-based online portal for content management, with numerous graphics and quizzes to help learning. Classes can also be done online via Microsoft Teams whenever necessary. Moreover, the teacher works effectively with ChatGPT to generate exercises and key vocabulary lists from lengthy transcripts.

However, if technology helps that much, I&mdash;someone who can independently work on the tools&mdash;think, "What's the point of travelling more than an hour daily to physically join this 'digitized classroom'?"

Second, there is a chance of contextual mismatch.

The content of our French class is primarily tailored for newcomers to Canada, and we learn a lot of vocabulary and expressions in realistic scenarios (e.g., Canadian community service and citizenship). It is a good example of cultural adaptation of learning experiences. This also serves well for the purpose of the [government-funded program](https://www.canada.ca/en/immigration-refugees-citizenship/services/settle-canada/language-skills/classes.html).

That said, learners are not equally "new" to the country, and each of us has our own needs and seeds, as well as varying understanding of the context we're currently in. I personally found the situational aspect of the course boring and demotivating. Instead, learning [how to order a coffee at a cafe](https://coffeebreaklanguages.com/tag/cbf-season-1/?order=asc) and listening to [current affairs](https://innerfrench.com/podcast) in French stimulate and motivate me more than following one-size-fits-all curricula.

Third, forced participation is not for everyone.

Our class often provides us opportunities to learn from games and interactive feedback. I sometimes feel uncomfortable with them because it seems participation is "obligated" in the environment, as if being told, "If you don't speak up, you are bad." Some will enjoy it, while others won't.

Not to mention, when there is a greater degree of agency among participants, the room can easily become competitive; those who are willing to participate will receive better recognition from teachers and parents, and we unconsciously play this attention-seeking game while losing the original joy and motivation for the subject itself. (That's why I hate anything competitive.)

Ultimately, learning shouldn't be for short-term efficiency, usefulness, pleasure, or achievements. Rather, it needs to be a sustainable journey that continues for years or a lifetime. It doesn't make sense if I become conversational in French in the next few months but forget everything after I complete the class.

Overall, the French class has undoubtedly been one of the most well-developed in-class experiences I've ever had. However, by squeezing everyone into the same room and asking them to follow a prescribed format, the one-to-many education setup has posed its structural limitations.

Thus, pedagogical correctness doesn't guarantee whether an educational program works for a certain person or not.

There is no silver bullet.

Even if everyone followed best practices with good intentions at a micro level, gaps and inequalities will emerge as soon as multiple agents&mdash;people&mdash;start interacting with each other and forming a system. No two people are the same!

Thus, it'd be essential to provide options when it comes to teaching and learning. I didn't find modern pedagogy helpful in my French classroom, but the same format can work perfectly for other subjects. Or I may simply enjoy receiving one-way lectures and reflecting independently, and deeply, at home. Meanwhile, you may need a 1:1 tutor, not a group teacher.

That is the personalization problem. In [his 2017 interview](https://dataskeptic.com/blog/episodes/2017/recommender-systems-live-from-farcon) (around 37'15), famous scholar Joseph Konstan raised education as one of the most underdeveloped domains in recommender systems; even though teaching methods and practices have been diversified, many classrooms still progress sequentially without much customization, excluding those who cannot follow the "normal" route.

It's not to say which teaching method is "better" or "worse." Since everything can potentially work, we need to first understand learners' context at the right granularity. And a tailored learning path needs to be co-developed with them.
